Building a Barbie Dream Elevator!

Posted by Leran Hu, Lindsey Sands, Natalie Arce, and Vanessa Lopez on May 5, 2026

Every Barbie Dream House needs an elevator.

Inclusivity is part of the mission of Mattel, as we've seen an increasing number of new Barbie dolls that exhibit physical differences. In 2019, for example, Mattel released a line of dolls with disabilities, some being wheelchair users, and others having prosthetic legs. With these additions, it only makes sense to renovate all Barbie Dream Houses to make sure they are ADA compliant. We hope to design a proof of concept for elevator installations for Barbie Dream Houses.

Introduction and Background

The goal of this project was to create a small-scale elevator that could transport Barbie Dolls about three feet up and down. Typical Barbie Dreamhouses sold on the market are at least three floors which puts them in the category of buildings required by ADA guidelines to have an elevator.

Some Barbie dreamhouses do have elevators, but they are not autonomous. They require girls playing to manually move the elevator up and down which can be less fun for girls with hand- or arm-related physical disabilities. They also look unlike real elevators and can sometimes cause confusion.

Most elevators are controlled either by a hydraulic system or a pulley system. For our project, considering the scale of what is being lifted up, we chose to create a pulley controlled elevator. This is done by implementing a motor that can be controlled and using a counterweight to stabilize the pulley system holding up the elevator cabin.

Requirements and Specifications

In order to create our elevator, choices were made based on a realistic set of functionality, and other features that were considered a stretch goal.

  • The ability of the elevator to move up and down with a maximum displacement of 40 inches.
  • The ability for the elevator to hold up at least 3lb of weight in addition to the weight of the elevator itself
  • The ability to move between different floors without human force.
  • Look like a life-size elevator
  • Be able to brake in an emergency

Types of specifications: Motor specifications, gear specifications, pulley specifications based on the motor, determining tension of wire, counterweight specifications

The typical Barbie doll is 11-12" tall, so we decided to make the elevator 13" tall to accommodate for the tallest barbies and give them a little bit of head room. We wanted a minimum of 3 floors since, as mentioned above, most Barbie dreamhouses have three floors. Additionally, the three floors are what mandated the installation of an elevator as per ADA guidelines.

We wanted the Barbies to be able to fit in the elevator with their friends, so we want a maximum carrying capcity of 3 lb. Most Barbie dolls are less than 0.5 lb, so 6 dolls should be able to comfortably ride in the elevator together.

We also mentioned above that we want the elevator to not only be accessible for dolls, but for the girls playing with them as well. Having an elevator that can move with the push of a button will remove te challenge for girls with physical disabilities that prevent them from being able to lift the elevator themselves. The other requirement is that the elevator resembles real life elevators more, as current Barbie Dreamhouse elevators do not look very realistic.

One final stretch goal is that the system be able to brake in case of an emergency. Most elevators have emergency braking systems in case something fails and puts passengers in danger. We care about the safety of our passengers, so we want to put mechanisms in place that will ensure a safe ride.

Design Features of Main Subsystems

Design is a huge part of creating safe elevators.

Motor-Gear-Pulley Subsystem

We had to calculate the necessary torques for lifting the system in order to choose a proper motor, pulley, and gear ratio.

Torque Calculations

In order to lift the elevator and its contents, the motor would have to be able to exert enough torque. We chose the BRINGSMART 12V 160rpm DC Worm Gear Motor , as it has a rated torque of 0.723 ft-lb.

We collected data to further characterize the motor. Using a multimeter, we found the resistance, and using a multimeter, we measured the no-load speed at varying voltages. From this information, we were able to create the torque-speed curve below:

Since the elevator box and its supports weigh about 8lb, we chose a pulley of 3 in. in diameter to make the torque to lift the elevator by itself 1 ft-lb. This torque is greater than the rated torque of the motor, so we added a 2.5 lb counterweight to remove some of the torque responsibility of the motor.

Gear Calculations

Despite the pulley diameter and counterweight working together to meet the specifications, we added a gear ratio to increase the torque and subsequently, the factor of safety. We care about the lives of our dolls and don't want them to get injured, so we wanted to make the factor of safety relatively high.

We added a gear ratio of 5:1 to apply a 5x increase in magnitude of the torque of the motor. For budget purposes, we ended up purchasing a 15-tooth gear and a 76-tooth gear from McMaster-Carr.

Future Work

In the future, we want to add some additional features to make our elevator more life-like. We would add elevator doors. The doors could be on each floor or on the elevator itself, but they would open when prompted and then close after some allotted amount of time to close the dolls in as they travel up and down. We could also add physical buttons for users to press instead of entering the desired floor into the computer.

Additionally, since this project was only a proof of concept, we could begin to investigate how this system could be more cost effective. We could investigate how we could replace certain parts so it could begin to be implemented in real Barbie Dreamhouses. We could also edit the geometries for manufacturability.
